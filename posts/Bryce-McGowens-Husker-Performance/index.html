Bryce McGowens: Was he the Husker he should have been?

basketball
huskers
NCAA
sports
Author

Kate Cordes

Published

May 6, 2023

Bryce McGowens was the first and only 5-star recruit Husker Hoops has ever landed. He was the player destined to save Fred Hoiberg’s tenure at Nebraska. While Hoiberg has stuck around in Lincoln, it is not due to the 2020-2021 season. Instead, the season was a disappointment, as McGowens carried the Cornhuskers to a pitiful 10-22 season record.

The five-star was destined for greatness, but did he live up to the expectations? Did Husker fans simply expect too much of this 18-year-old?

Following Nebraska’s 2021-2022 season, McGowens declared for the NBA draft, being selected in the second round by the Minnesota Timberwolves. He was traded to the Charlotte Hornets and has been a consistent contributor on both their G League and NBA teams in the 2022-2023 season, leaving Husker fans to wonder if he played to his full potential in Lincoln.

This article will analyze his offensive contributions only. Of course, that ignores half of the game. We will focus on the offensive half because McGowens was regarded as an elite scorer as a recruit. McGowens showed glimmers of greatness on offense, but do his numbers show it?

McGowens played significantly more minutes than any of his teammates, as the Hoiberg did not have a very deep squad. Often times he had the ball in his hands, so it would make sense for him to score much and often. Clearly, there is a positive coorelation between minutes played and points scored. The more minutes a player plays, the more opportunities they have to score, and, generally, the more points they score.

However, just because McGowens played and scored a lot, it doesn’t mean he scored efficiently. Let’s take a look at his field goal percentage, or total percentage of made shots.

McGowens shot the ball with a field goal percentage of .403. As depicted above, he shot the ball a lot. His only teammate that was even close to him was Alonzo Verge, a Husker who was consistently labeled a ball hog.

Of Huskers who played at least 150 minutes, only four players shot the ball at a lower clip than McGowens. That is not exactly a stat you want to see out of the face of your program. So, why did McGowens keep shooting the ball if other Huskers were more consistent than he was?

Perhaps he did not want to share the ball.

McGowens did not like sharing the ball. He wanted to shoot. Among all Big Ten guards who played at least the average number of minutes, 468, and recorded at least 40 turnovers, he was one of four players with more turnovers than assists. He turned the ball over 65 times. Of these guards, he recorded the second fewest assists with 43. When he had the ball, he looked to score. This is not inherently bad, but when other players are scoring more consitently, many Husker fans wondered why he didn’t pass more often.

Maybe fans were too critical. Maybe guards just shoot at a lower clip beacuse they have the ball often and shoot more. Let’s look at McGowens compared to the rest of the Big Ten.

Among Big Ten players who played at least the average number of minutes, 468, McGowens finished with a fairly low made-shot rate. Looking at each position, we see that, generally, centers shoot at the highest rate, followed by forwards, with guards coming in last.

Within the position group with the lowest percentage in general, McGowens still finished near the bottom. Among Big Ten freshmen, represented as black dots, he also finished near the bottom.

This is interesting to note, as many Husker fans were outraged when he was not awarded the Big Ten Freshman of the Year award. Let’s take a look at how he compares to other Big Ten freshmen offensively.

So, why didn’t McGowens win the award? As we have already established, points scored does not necessarily constitue an elite player.

When we compare a few of McGowens and Branham’s key offensive statistics, we can see that Branham shot and shared the ball much more consistently than McGowens did. Really, it is not that surprising that Branham won the award ahead of McGowens. His team was more successful than the Huskers, and he simply put up better stats.

It is difficult to analyze whether McGowens was the player he was promised to be. He was a freshmen leading a rag-tag team who didn’t play together and suffered several injuries throughout the season. He scored as he was promised to, but he didn’t pass as he was promised to.

Regardless of records and statistics, McGowens always put on a show: something that had been misisng from Nebraska basketball for a few seasons. Even in blow out defeats, fans could count on a monstrous dunk or big three pointer to get excited about. He also proved that Nebraska can prepare players for the NBA. A couple former Huskers are hanging around the NBA currently, and McGowens just gives the fans another alumnus to cheer on.

No matter how lauded or criticized he was during his time in Lincoln, fans just want to see him do well and make the state proud. It seems as though he is on his way to doing just that in Charlotte.
